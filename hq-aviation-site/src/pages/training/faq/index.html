Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about learning to fly helicopters.

Getting Started

The best way to start is with a trial lesson. This gives you a genuine taste of helicopter flying with no obligation. If you enjoy it, we'll discuss the full PPL course and create a training plan that fits your schedule and goals.

Medical Requirements

For a PPL(H), you need a Class 2 medical certificate. This is obtained from a CAA-approved Aviation Medical Examiner (AME). The examination covers general health, vision, hearing, and blood pressure. For most people, it's straightforward.
Yes! Wearing glasses or contact lenses is not a problem. You simply need to carry a spare pair when flying. Vision requirements are based on corrected vision, so as long as your vision is good with glasses/contacts, you're fine.
Some forms of colour vision deficiency may limit your flying. However, many people with colour blindness can still fly. If you fail the standard test, there are additional tests that may allow you to fly with certain restrictions.
Many medical conditions that you might think would prevent flying are actually compatible with holding a pilot's license. Diabetes, heart conditions, and mental health conditions are assessed on a case-by-case basis. If you're uncertain, contact us or consult with an AME before starting training.

PPL Course

The minimum flight time is 45 hours. Most students complete in 50-60 hours. Calendar time depends on how often you fly: 3-4 months if intensive, 6-12 months flying once or twice a week. Weather also affects scheduling.
Based on minimum hours (45), expect approximately £18,000-£22,000 total including flight training, ground school, medical, exams, and the skills test. Most students require 50-60 hours, which increases the cost. We offer various payment options and recommend budgeting for 55 hours.
Training includes flight instruction (hover work, circuits, navigation, emergencies) and ground school (9 theoretical subjects). You'll fly dual with an instructor, then solo, and complete a qualifying cross-country flight before the skills test.
A PPL allows you to fly helicopters for private purposes - travel, leisure, and sharing costs with passengers (not for profit). You cannot fly commercially without further training for a CPL. It's also the foundation for all advanced ratings and professional licenses.

Practical Questions

We recommend at least once a week, ideally twice. Flying less frequently means more time spent re-learning skills, which costs more overall. Intensive courses (3-4 times per week) are efficient but require a flexible schedule.
Helicopter training requires reasonable visibility. If weather prevents flying, we'll reschedule your lesson at no extra cost. This is why flexible scheduling helps - if you can fly at short notice on good days, you'll progress faster.
Comfortable clothing and flat shoes (no high heels). The helicopter can get warm in summer, so layers are useful. Sunglasses are helpful but avoid polarized lenses as they can interfere with screen visibility.
Modern helicopter flying, with proper training and well-maintained aircraft, is very safe. Robinson helicopters have an excellent safety record when operated within their parameters. Our rigorous training and maintenance standards ensure the highest safety levels.

Career Pathways

Yes! After your PPL, you can progress to a Commercial Pilot License (CPL) which requires a minimum of 155 hours flight time and a Class 1 medical. From there, career options include charter, aerial work, flight instruction, and more.
After CPL, you complete a Flight Instructor (FI) course. This requires 200 hours total time and teaches you how to instruct others. It's a popular career path that allows you to build hours while earning.
Commercial helicopter careers include: VIP/executive charter, flight instruction, aerial photography/filming, survey work, agricultural operations, police/air ambulance (usually requiring significant experience), offshore transport, and utility operations.
